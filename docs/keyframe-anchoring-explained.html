DPS-SLAM · Design Note

Where Do Detections
Get Attached?

How the single-graph and dual-graph approaches tie gate and marker sightings to the pose graph — and why both attach measurements to slightly wrong poses.

1

The Setup: Breadcrumbs and Sightings

As the drone flies, it drops a keyframe — think of it as a breadcrumb — every 2 meters of travel. The map is built only out of these breadcrumbs. This rule is identical in both versions: seeing a gate or marker never creates a keyframe by itself.

When the drone spots a gate, the measurement is inherently relative: “the gate is 3 m ahead of me and 1 m to my left.” That statement is only true from the exact spot where the drone was looking. To use it, the system must tie it to one of the breadcrumbs — but the drone almost never sees a gate at the precise moment it drops one. The sighting happens somewhere in between.

🎯

The core question: the sighting happened between breadcrumbs. Which breadcrumb does the measurement get glued to — and does anyone correct for the gap? Each version answers this differently.

2

Single Graph errs backward

The moment a gate is seen, the single graph immediately attaches the measurement to the last breadcrumb already dropped — the one behind the drone, possibly up to 2 meters back.

It's like saying: “from the lamppost I passed a while ago, the gate is 3 m ahead and 1 m left.” That's false — it was 3 m ahead of the drone, which kept flying after the lamppost. The distance traveled since that breadcrumb is silently absorbed as error in where the map believes the gate is.

Two mitigations soften this. While gates are in view, the breadcrumb spacing tightens from 2 m to 0.5 m, shrinking the worst-case gap. And a throttle keeps only one sighting per object per breadcrumb interval, limiting how much inconsistent evidence piles up.

3

Dual Graph errs forward

The dual graph starts more carefully. While the drone is seeing gates, it keeps a small private scratch map on the side, dropping mini-breadcrumbs every 20 cm. Each sighting is tied to a mini-breadcrumb at most 20 cm from the true viewing spot — nearly exact.

But the scratch map is temporary. When the drone hits the distance threshold and drops the next real breadcrumb, everything in the scratch map is squashed into a single summarized statement and attached to that brand-new breadcrumb — the one dropped after the gate was seen.

💡

This is exactly the behavior you were told about: the pose is added at step k+1, but the gate information inside it was gathered at step k. The drone may have moved up to half a meter between “saw the gate” and “dropped the breadcrumb,” and that gap is baked into the merged constraint as if it didn't exist.

There's a second, subtler cost: the merge squashes many individual sightings into one constraint. The rich per-sighting geometry that the scratch map captured so carefully is collapsed before it ever reaches the real map.

5

So… Is This the Right Behavior?

No — in both versions it's an approximation error, not a defensible design choice. The textbook-correct options would be to either drop a keyframe at the exact pose where the sighting happened, or mathematically shift the measurement so it's expressed relative to the keyframe it gets attached to. Neither version does either one: both glue a measurement to a pose it was not measured from.

What can be said in its defense: the error is bounded and known — it can never exceed the breadcrumb spacing — and both versions deliberately shrink that bound by tightening the spacing to 0.5 m whenever gates are in view. That tightening is the author's chosen mitigation, in place of fixing the geometry.

And the benchmark result makes sense in this light: the single graph wins in practice because the dual graph's extra machinery — the summary step, the squashing of sightings, plus its own forward anchoring gap — costs more accuracy than its careful scratch map saves.

The one-sentence answer Both versions knowingly attach detections to slightly wrong poses — the single graph errs backward toward the previous keyframe, the dual graph errs forward at merge time — neither is geometrically correct, but the error is bounded by the keyframe spacing, which is why that spacing tightens when detections appear.
